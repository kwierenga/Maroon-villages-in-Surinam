Bibliografie & Bronnen

This page lists the primary academic works, legal documents, institutional reports, encyclopaedic sources, and online references used throughout this site. It is a working document — additions and corrections are welcome.

A note on sources. The history and present situation of Surinamese Maroons have been documented by a relatively small number of dedicated scholars — above all Richard Price and Sally Price, whose decades of fieldwork with the Saramaka and their broader comparative work underpin much of what is known in English. Dutch-language scholarship (Thoden van Velzen, Kobben, van Wetering) is comparably significant but less accessible to an English-language audience. For current events and land rights, the Minority Rights Group and Cultural Survival maintain the most consistently updated secondary sources. Legal primary sources — IACtHR judgements in particular — are publicly available and authoritative on specific events. Wikipedia is used throughout this site as a pointer to sources, not as a primary source itself.

Legal & Human Rights Documents

Primary legal sources. These documents are publicly available and authoritative on specific events and rights.

Inter-American Court of Human Rights. Case of the Saramaka People v. Suriname. Judgment of November 28, 2007. Series C No. 172.

IACtHRPrimary SourceLand Rights

Landmark ruling determining that the Saramaka constitute a tribal people with collective rights over their ancestral territory. Sets precedent for all Maroon and Indigenous peoples in the Americas. Lists the 12 Saramaka clans and their villages by name.

Full judgment (PDF) →

Inter-American Court of Human Rights. Case of the Moiwana Community v. Suriname. Judgment of June 15, 2005. Series C No. 124.

IACtHRPrimary SourceMoiwana

Found Suriname guilty of human rights violations in the 1986 Moiwana massacre. Ordered reparations to survivors, criminal investigation, and a public ceremony of state apology.

Full judgment (PDF) →

Peace Treaty between the Dutch Colonial Authority and the Ndyuka Maroons. October 10, 1760.

TreatyPrimary SourceNdyuka

The first Maroon peace treaty. Granted the Ndyuka territorial autonomy in the upper Tapanahoni basin. The date, 10 October, is now the national Day of the Maroons in Suriname. Full text reproduced in Price (1983).
