The Moiwana Massacre

29 November 1986 · Moiwana Village, Marowijne District, Suriname

On the night of 29 November 1986, units of the Surinamese National Army descended on the Ndyuka Maroon village of Moiwana. Over four hours, soldiers went house to house — killing at least 38 unarmed civilians, mostly women and children, and burning the village to the ground. The perpetrators were never prosecuted. The survivors fled across the Marowijne River into French Guiana. Nearly twenty years later, the Inter-American Court of Human Rights found the Surinamese state guilty of the killings and ordered reparations. The case remains a defining moment in Maroon history and in the broader history of human rights in the Americas.

Background — Suriname in 1986

Suriname had been under military rule since a coup led by Dési Bouterse in February 1980. By 1982, Bouterse had executed fifteen prominent civilian critics — journalists, lawyers, academics — in what became known as the December Murders, prompting international condemnation and the suspension of Dutch aid. The country's economy was deteriorating and its international position was increasingly isolated.

Within this climate, a young Ndyuka Maroon named Ronnie Brunswijk — who had served as Bouterse's personal bodyguard before the two had a falling out — assembled a guerrilla force drawn largely from Ndyuka and Saramaka men. This force, the Surinamese Liberation Army, better known as the Jungle Commando, began attacking military installations in eastern Suriname in the summer of 1986. The military government responded not only against the fighters but against Maroon civilians throughout the eastern interior.

The central cause of Maroon participation in the uprising was not simply a personal feud between Bouterse and Brunswijk. It was rooted in the long-standing contempt with which successive Surinamese governments had treated Maroon communities — denying them legal recognition of their land rights, failing to deliver basic services, and treating the interior as a resource-extraction zone rather than a homeland.

The Night of 29 November 1986

Moiwana was a small Ndyuka village near the east-west road connecting Paramaribo with Albina, roughly ten kilometres west of Albina. It was the home community of Ronnie Brunswijk, and the military identified it as a Jungle Commando base — though by the time of the operation, Brunswijk had already relocated his command elsewhere in the forest.

Army units arrived by helicopter. They sealed the roads in and out of the village. Over the next four hours, soldiers moved house to house. What followed was documented by survivors, later investigated by the United Nations, and ultimately adjudicated by the Inter-American Court of Human Rights.

Everyone was shot — the unarmed women, pregnant women, a baby barely seven months old. No distinctions were made.

— Memre Moiwana, testimony compiled by the survivor NGO Moiwana '86

At least 38 people were killed. Some estimates, including those cited by the U.S. State Department in the weeks following, placed the death toll significantly higher. The victims were shot, stabbed with machetes, and in some accounts dismembered. The bodies were doused in petrol and thrown into pits. The village was burned. Survivors who escaped into the surrounding forest eventually made their way across the Marowijne River into French Guiana.

In the weeks following, the violence extended to other Ndyuka communities along the Cottica River region. The U.S. State Department reported 244 Ndyuka killed in December 1986 alone. A United Nations investigator entering the area months later found "no human being or living creature" in one town, with jungle vegetation already reclaiming destroyed buildings.

Flight and Displacement

By early 1987, approximately 10,000 Ndyuka had crossed the Marowijne River into French Guiana. French authorities recognised them as refugees but confined them to camps surrounded by barbed wire. Others found shelter with relatives in Paramaribo. Many Saramaka, less directly targeted but also fleeing the war, crossed into French Guiana as undocumented migrants, hiding from French authorities charged with expelling illegal border-crossers.

The displacement was not temporary. Many Ndyuka remained in French Guiana for years; some settled there permanently. The refugee camps were not closed until 1992 when the Interior War formally ended, and even then, the return was not straightforward — communities had been scattered, villages destroyed, and the political and legal situation that had triggered the uprising remained unresolved.

Whole communities in the Cottica River region — the area most heavily targeted — never fully reconstituted. Some villages that existed before November 1986 no longer exist today.

The Survivors and Moiwana '86

Survivors and the relatives of those killed did not accept impunity. In the years following the massacre, a group of survivors established the NGO Moiwana '86, which worked to document the killings, preserve testimony, and pursue accountability. Their evidence-gathering — conducted at considerable personal risk during an active civil war — became the foundation of the eventual legal case.

A police inspector named Herman Eddy Gooding, who had the courage to investigate the massacres while the war was still ongoing, was found shot dead in 1990. His death was never conclusively resolved.

The memorial inaugurated at Moiwana in 2007, on the 21st anniversary of the massacre, was designed by a local artist based in nearby Moengo. The central structure incorporates inscriptions in the Afaka script — a syllabary invented by the Ndyuka man Afaka Atumisi in 1910 to write the Ndyuka language. Its use at the memorial is a deliberate assertion of Ndyuka identity and cultural continuity in the face of an act that sought to destroy a community.

The Legal Process — IACtHR 2005

In 2000, survivors of the Moiwana massacre filed a petition with the Inter-American Commission on Human Rights. The case was referred to the Inter-American Court of Human Rights and a judgement was handed down on 15 June 2005.

The Court found the Surinamese state guilty of the extra-judicial execution of members of the Moiwana community and the subsequent forced displacement of survivors. The judgement also upheld the 1760 Ndyuka peace treaty and determined that "the Moiwana community members may be considered the legitimate owners of their traditional lands" — language with significant implications for Maroon land rights more broadly.

The Court ordered Suriname to:

  • Pay reparations of approximately US$3 million to the survivors and victims' relatives
  • Finance the construction of new housing for community members who wished to return
  • Conduct a full criminal investigation into the killings
  • Hold a public ceremony at which the Surinamese state recognises its responsibility and formally apologises to the Ndyuka people
  • Establish a community development fund for the Moiwana community

The reparations were paid, and the memorial was built. The criminal investigation and the formal public apology have not been completed as of 2026. No individual has been prosecuted for the killings.

Significance

The Moiwana massacre was not an isolated event. It was the most documented single incident in a broader campaign of violence against Maroon communities during the 1986–1992 Interior War — a war whose root cause was the failure of the Surinamese state to recognise the rights, autonomy, and dignity of Maroon people who had been free for more than two centuries before Suriname itself was independent.

The IACtHR judgement is legally significant beyond Moiwana itself. By upholding the 1760 Ndyuka peace treaty, the Court affirmed that those original instruments of Maroon sovereignty retain legal force. By finding that the Moiwana community members are "legitimate owners" of their traditional lands, it created precedent that was directly relevant to the 2007 Saramaka territorial rights ruling two years later.

For the Ndyuka and for all Surinamese Maroons, Moiwana represents both the worst of what they have endured and the resilience with which they have pursued justice across decades. The memorial in the clearing by the road is a physical statement that the event is not forgotten, that the community continues, and that the demand for accountability has not been withdrawn.

Afaka Script

The Afaka script is a syllabary invented in 1910 by Afaka Atumisi, an Ndyuka man, to write the Ndyuka language. It is one of the very few scripts independently invented in the Western Hemisphere. Its use at the Moiwana memorial is a deliberate cultural and political statement.

Afaka claimed to have received the script in a vision. Despite initial scepticism, the script spread among Ndyuka communities and remains in use today.
