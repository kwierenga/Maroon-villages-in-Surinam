Languages of the Suriname Maroons

Creole Languages, Their Origins, and Their Place in the World

The Maroon peoples of Suriname and French Guiana speak some of the most linguistically remarkable languages in the Americas. Born from the violent collision of West and Central African tongues with English, Portuguese, and Dutch on the plantation system of the 17th and 18th centuries, the Surinamese Maroon creoles are fully developed, grammatically complete languages — native tongues passed from parent to child for over three centuries. They are not broken English or simplified Dutch. They are languages in their own right, and they carry within them traces of their African origins that no other language family in the Western Hemisphere preserves as deeply.

What Is a Pidgin Language?

A pidgin arises when speakers of two or more mutually unintelligible languages are forced into regular contact — most commonly in situations of trade, colonial labour, or slavery — and improvise a shared means of communication. A pidgin is nobody's mother tongue: it is a second language for everyone who uses it, with simplified grammar, limited vocabulary, and variable pronunciation depending on the speaker's native language background. Pidgins are not inferior languages; they are practical solutions to specific communication problems. They emerge wherever populations with no shared language are thrown together: West African trading posts, Pacific colonial ports, Caribbean plantations.

Examples of historic pidgins include the original Chinese Pidgin English of the Canton trade, the Chinook Jargon of the Pacific Northwest, and the various Plantation Pidgins of the Caribbean and South America that eventually gave rise to today's creole languages. The Ndyuka–Trio Pidgin — a contact language between Ndyuka Maroons and Tiriyó Amerindians in the Suriname interior — is a rare example of a pidgin that arose between two non-European communities.

What Is a Creole Language?

A creole emerges when a pidgin becomes the first language of a new generation of children. When children grow up hearing a pidgin as their primary linguistic environment, they do not merely repeat the simplified adult speech: they expand it. A child's brain, equipped with what linguists call the language acquisition device, automatically adds grammatical structure, regularises verb forms, expands vocabulary, and creates a fully articulated language. This process — called creolisation — typically occurs within a single generation.

The result is a language that may share much of its vocabulary with its "parent" European language (called the superstrate or lexifier) but diverges dramatically in grammar, phonology, and meaning — often reflecting the grammar patterns of the African substrate languages from which enslaved workers came. Saramaccan, for instance, has approximately 50% African vocabulary when ritual speech is included, and its tonal system mirrors West African languages rather than English or Portuguese. Ndyuka's serial verb constructions — stringing verbs together without conjunctions — are a direct survival of West African grammatical patterns.

Linguists estimate that roughly 100 creole languages have arisen since 1500, the majority in the Atlantic world as a direct result of the slave trade. They include Haitian Creole (French-based, 12 million speakers), Jamaican Patois (English-based), Papiamento of the ABC islands (mixed Spanish/Portuguese), Cape Verdean Creole (Portuguese-based), and the full family of Surinamese creoles. Far from being curiosities, these are living languages used daily by tens of millions of people worldwide.

The Atlantic Creoles: Context and Family

The Surinamese creoles belong to the family of Atlantic English-based creoles, which share structural features derived from their common African substrate — the Kwa languages of the Gold Coast (Akan, Fon, Ewe) and the Bantu languages of Central Africa (KiKongo, Kimbundu). These substrate languages contributed not only vocabulary but fundamental grammar: tonal distinctions, serial verb constructions, aspect markers rather than tense markers, and the use of preverbal particles to indicate time and mood.

What makes the Surinamese creoles — particularly Saramaccan and Ndyuka — distinct from other Atlantic English-based creoles is their extraordinary degree of African retention. Because the Maroons escaped from the plantations so early (c.1690–1712) and lived in complete isolation from European contact for over a century, their languages were not subject to the gradual "decreolisation" process that brought Jamaican Patois, Barbadian Bajan, or even Sranan Tongo closer to their English lexifier over time. Saramaccan and Ndyuka are therefore considered the most conservative and "deep" of all Atlantic creoles — the closest living languages to what was spoken on the 17th-century Suriname plantations.

The Surinamese Maroon creoles are related to — but distinct from — Sranan Tongo, the coastal creole that serves as Suriname's lingua franca. Sranan Tongo diverged from the same Plantation Creole root around 1690–1700, but remained in contact with Dutch and English and has thus incorporated more European vocabulary and grammatical features over the centuries. Today Sranan Tongo is spoken by approximately 300,000–400,000 people in Suriname as a first or second language, and by significant numbers in the Netherlands.

Ndyuka (Aukan / Okanisi)

ISO 639: djk
Speakers50,000–90,000
BranchEastern Maroon Creole
Lexifier sourcesEnglish (46%), Dutch (16%), Portuguese (35%), African (3% documented, higher in ritual)
Spoken byNdyuka, Aluku, Paramaka peoples; Suriname and French Guiana
ScriptLatin alphabet (modern English-based orthography since 1984); Afaka syllabary (1910)
TonalYes — tone is phonemic (na = "is"; ná = "is not")
StatusStable but unrecognised officially; not a language of school instruction in Suriname

Ndyuka is an English-based creole with strong African grammatical features. Serial verb constructions — e.g. a teki a nefi koti a brede ("he took the knife cut the bread") — reflect Kwa/Gbe grammar patterns. The language eliminates the letter 'r' (present in Sranan Tongo), and uses prenasalised consonants such as mb and ng common in West and Central Africa. Words can begin with kp and gb, consonant clusters absent from European languages. Tone is phonemically significant but rarely written. The Afaka syllabary — a 56-character system — was invented for Ndyuka in 1910 and remains in limited but continued use. SIL International produced an Aukan–English dictionary (Shanks, 2000) with 1,764 entries, and George and Mary Huttar's comprehensive grammar (1994) remains the standard scholarly reference.

Written Sample

En so den be abaa na a líba, dísi wi kai Kawína Líba. Di den abaa de, den abaa teke gwe na opu fu Kawína. En so den be waka langa langa gwe te na Mama Ndyuka ede, pe wi kai Mama Ndyuka.

And so they crossed the river, which we call "Kawina [Commewijne] River". Having crossed it, they went way upstream along the Commewijne. Thus they travelled a long, long way, clear to the upper Tapanahony, the place we call "Mama Ndyuka".

Source: SIL International / Wikipedia: Ndyuka language

Saramaccan (Saamáka)

ISO 639: srm
Speakers~58,000 in Suriname; 25,000 in French Guiana; 8,000 in Netherlands
BranchCentral Maroon Creole
Lexifier sourcesEnglish (30%), Portuguese (20%), African languages (50% including ritual)
Spoken bySaramaka and Matawai peoples; Suriname, French Guiana, Netherlands
ScriptLatin alphabet (SIL orthography, 1982; not universally adopted)
TonalYes — tonal system similar to West African languages; one tone per vowel
StatusEndangered; classified as vulnerable; school instruction absent in interior

Saramaccan is linguistically unique: it is the only creole in the world with two European lexifiers (English and Portuguese) at roughly comparable proportions. The Portuguese component — words like mujee (mulher, "woman"), womi (homem, "man"), da (dar, "to give"), bunu (bom, "good") — reflects the language of plantation overseers, many of whom were Sephardic Jewish merchants from Brazil who managed plantations and spoke Portuguese. The African component, at approximately 50% including ceremonial usage, is the highest of any creole in the Americas. Saramaccan phonology — including its tonal system — is directly parallel to West African languages rather than European ones. The Instituut voor Taalwetenschap (SIL Netherlands) produced a Saramaccan–English–Dutch wordlist (2000), a grammar sketch (Rountree, 1982/1992), and a New Testament translation. Two books by Richard Price have been translated into Saramaccan.

Written Sample

De waka te de aan sinkii möön. U ta mindi kanda fu dee soni dee ta pasa ku u. A suku di soni te wojo fëën ko bëë.

"They walked until they were worn out." "We make up songs about things that happen to us." "He searched for it in vain."

Source: SIL International Saramaccan–English Dictionary

Sranan Tongo

ISO 639: srn
Speakers300,000–400,000 (as first or second language in Suriname)
BranchAtlantic English-based Creole; Suriname coastal creole
Lexifier sourcesEnglish (majority), Dutch, Portuguese, African languages
Spoken bySurinamese population broadly; significant use in Netherlands diaspora
ScriptLatin alphabet; official spelling system established 1987
TonalNo — stress-based, not tonal
StatusVigorous; de facto lingua franca of Suriname though Dutch is official

Sranan Tongo ("Surinamese tongue") is the shared lingua franca of Suriname's ethnically diverse population — used between Creoles, Maroons, Javanese, Hindustani, and other communities who lack a common language. It diverged from the same Plantation Creole ancestor as Ndyuka and Saramaccan around 1690–1700, but has remained in continuous contact with Dutch and English and has thus incorporated more European vocabulary over time, making it less "deep" than the Maroon creoles. Key Portuguese retentions include pikin (pequeno, "small/child"), sabi (saber, "to know"), and bun (bom, "good/well"). Written since the 18th century — the earliest known texts date to the 1750s — Sranan has a longer written tradition than most creoles. It was suppressed under Dutch colonial rule but regained prestige after independence (1975) and particularly after Desi Bouterse began making speeches in it in the 1980s.

Written Sample

Ala den man gebore fri èn leki wan ini grani èn den leti. Den ben gi den rutu èn konsensi. Den musu abi lespeki gi ibriwan.

All human beings are born free and equal in dignity and rights. They are endowed with reason and conscience and should act towards one another in a spirit of brotherhood.

Source: Article 1, Universal Declaration of Human Rights (Sranan Tongo version)

Matawai

ISO 639: mbt
Speakers~8,500
BranchCentral Maroon Creole (related to Saramaccan)
Lexifier sourcesEnglish and Portuguese base, with African substrate
Spoken byMatawai people; upper Saramacca River, Suriname
ScriptLatin (limited); no standardised orthography in wide use
TonalYes
StatusEndangered — considered vulnerable by linguists; high rate of code-switching with Sranan Tongo

Matawai is closely related to Saramaccan — the two groups share the Central Maroon Creole branch — but diverged after the Matawai signed a separate peace treaty with the Dutch in 1767, isolating their community on the upper Saramacca River. The language is often described as a dialect of Saramaccan, but Matawai speakers insist it is a distinct language. Edward C. Green conducted the primary ethnographic and linguistic fieldwork among the Matawai between 1970 and 1973. The language faces particular pressure from Sranan Tongo, and code-switching is common, particularly among younger speakers. The Matawai are the smallest of the Central branch groups and maintain few language documentation resources.

Written Sample

A suku di a goon fu en. Den be waka go a busi fu feni wáta.

"He looked for his land." "They went into the forest to find water."

Source: Adapted from SIL Saramaccan documentation; Matawai variant

Saramaccan in Print — The Works of Richard & Sally Price

For over fifty years, American anthropologists Richard and Sally Price lived and worked alongside the Saramaka people of Suriname, first arriving at the village of Dangogo on the upper Suriname River in 1966. Their body of work — more than twenty books and dozens of articles — constitutes the most comprehensive scholarly record of Saramaka history, oral tradition, visual art, and daily life in existence. Two of their books have been published in Saramaccan itself, making them among the very few works of literature written in this language.

Fesiten

Saramaccan edition of First-Time: The Historical Vision of an African American People

Richard Price, translated with Sally Price; Saramaccan refined by Vinije Haabo — Vents d'ailleurs, La Roque-d'Anthéron, 2013. ISBN 978-2-36413-038-8 — 256 pp.

Fesiten is the Saramaccan translation of Price's landmark 1983 work First-Time, published after the 2007 Inter-American Court of Human Rights ruling in favour of the Saramaka people. The Saramaka authorities (VSG) used part of their compensation award to commission the translation, so that their own people could read their history in their own language. The Saramaccan was refined by Vinije Haabo, a Saramaka linguist living in French Guiana who also compiled a Saramaccan dictionary at saamaka.com.

The word fesiten — the organising concept of the entire book — is defined by Price on page 14 in the Saramaka's own words: fesiten is the time of the ancestors, the knowledge of things that happened fifty or a hundred years ago. A Saramaka elder from Dangogo told Price directly: "Fesiten ta kii sembe" — "Fesiten can kill a person." Knowledge of the ancestors is not merely historical: it is spiritually charged, potentially dangerous, and must be transmitted with great care.

The book contains 202 numbered oral testimonies recorded by Price from Saramaka elders in the 1960s and 1970s, woven together with commentary drawing on Dutch colonial archives in The Hague. The first narrative (#1, recorded from the elder Otyutyu on 13 August 1976) tells the story of Lanu's escape from a plantation — how a woman was killed for bringing water to an enslaved man, and how Lanu fled into the forest and was received by the Amerindians. This is how the Saramaka remember their founding history: through individual acts of survival, resistance, and protection, each name remembered across three centuries.

Saramaccan text
Andi da "fesiten"?
Fesiten, hen da di ten u dee gaansembe. Fesiten tooka ku dee sondi dee pasa feifiteni ee na wan hondo yaa kaa. Biga fesiten hebi tumisi. Ya musu taki sondi feen hesi hesi. Hen da di lutu fu hii Saamaka sabi.
What is "fesiten"?
Fesiten is the time of the ancestors. Fesiten concerns things that happened fifty or one hundred years ago. Because fesiten is very heavy. You must speak of these things with great care. It is the root of all Saramaka knowledge.
Fesiten, p.14. © Richard Price & Sally Price / Vents d'ailleurs, 2013.

Boo Go a Kontukonde

Saramaccan edition of Two Evenings in Saramaka

Richard Price and Sally Price, translated with Vinije Haabo; musical transcriptions by Kenneth M. Bilby — Vents d'ailleurs, La Roque-d'Anthéron, 2016. ISBN 978-2-36413-184-2 — 113 pp.

Boo go a Kontukonde — literally "go to the story-world" — is the Saramaccan translation of Two Evenings in Saramaka (1991), which recorded two complete tale-telling wakes that Price and Sally Price witnessed at Dangogo in April 1968. A death-night, in Saramaka custom, is not merely a vigil: it is an elaborate multi-day ceremony involving specific ritual obligations, communal drumming, singing, and the telling of folktales — kontu — that continues through the night.

The book opens with Di dede fu Sindobobi — the death of Sindobobi — precisely dated to 29 April 1968, ten o'clock at night, when the Prices were woken in their hammocks by the cries of Asabosi, whose mother had just died after a long illness. The scene that follows — neighbours arriving in the dark, the body carried out and laid beneath a banana tree, a shotgun fired into the bush to drive off evil spirits — is recorded in the Saramaccan of those who lived it.

The book includes more than fifty songs in musical notation, transcribed by Kenneth Bilby. For those wishing to hear the actual recordings from which this book was drawn, Music from Saramaka: A Dynamic Afro-American Tradition (Smithsonian Folkways, 1977) contains recordings made by the Prices between 1967 and 1976 and is freely accessible at Smithsonian Folkways.

Saramaccan text
Di dede fu Sindobobi
29 april 1968. Tin yuu ndeti. U bi de na amaka kaa di u yei di kee fu Asabosi. A bi de neen pisi ka hen mma, Sindobobi, bi de ku lan siki sensi di meni yaa.
The death of Sindobobi
29 April 1968. Ten o'clock at night. We were in our hammocks when we heard the cries of Asabosi. She was nearby, where her mother Sindobobi had been gravely ill for many years.
Boo go a Kontukonde, p.19. © Richard Price & Sally Price / Vents d'ailleurs, 2016.
How to obtain these books: Both books are out of print. Their publisher, Vents d'ailleurs, ceased operations around 2020. The most direct route is to contact Richard and Sally Price via their website richandsally.net, who may have copies available or can advise. University libraries with Caribbean or Suriname collections — particularly the KITLV in Leiden (kitlv.nl) — may hold copies accessible via interlibrary loan. AbeBooks and WorldCat (worldcat.org) are also worth searching.

Other key works by Richard & Sally Price

First-Time: The Historical Vision of an African American People
1983 / 2nd ed. 2002, University of Chicago Press
The foundational oral history of the Saramaka resistance wars, presented in parallel with Dutch colonial documents. Winner of the Albert J. Beveridge Award and the J. I. Staley Prize in Anthropology.
Alabi's World
1990, Johns Hopkins University Press
An account of 18th-century Saramaka life told through multiple voices — Saramaka oral tradition, Dutch colonial records, and Moravian missionary diaries — in an experimental multi-perspective format.
Maroon Arts: Cultural Vitality in the African Diaspora
1999, Beacon Press (Dutch edition: De kunst van de Marrons, KIT Press, 2000)
The definitive study of Maroon visual art — woodcarving, textile, calabash art — covering three centuries of creative tradition across all six Maroon groups. Richly illustrated.
Rainforest Warriors: Human Rights on Trial
2011, University of Pennsylvania Press
The story of the 2007 IACtHR case Saramaka People v. Suriname, in which Price participated as an expert witness. Winner of the 2012 Best Book Award of the American Political Science Association in Human Rights.
Travels with Tooy: History, Memory, and the African American Imagination
2008, University of Chicago Press
Price's account of his relationship with Tooy, a Saramaka priest-philosopher living in French Guiana, spanning 35 years of fieldwork. Winner of the Victor Turner Prize.
Music from Saramaka: A Dynamic Afro-American Tradition
Smithsonian Folkways Recordings, 1977 (recorded 1967–1976)
Audio recordings made by the Prices during their years in Dangogo. Freely accessible at Smithsonian Folkways — the only authentic Saramaka audio in open public access.
