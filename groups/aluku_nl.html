Aluku

Also known as: Boni · Aluku Nenge · Opu Nenge

De Aluku — ook bekend als de Boni — voerden de langste weerstandsoorlog van enige Marron-groep tegen de Nederlandse koloniale autoriteit. Hun oorlogen eindigden pas in 1793 toen een alliantie van Nederlandse troepen en Ndyuka-vrijwilligers hen definitief versloeg. Hun kerngebied verschoof naar de Lawa aan de Surinaams-Frans-Guyana-grens, en tegenwoordig wonen de meeste Aluku in Frans-Guyana als Franse staatsburgers — de enige Marron-groep met EU-burgerschap.

History

The Aluku — better known in Suriname by the name Boni, after their most celebrated resistance leader — fought the longest and most intense resistance war of any Maroon group against the Dutch colonial authority. Where the Ndyuka and Saramaka had won peace treaties by the early 1760s, the Aluku continued fighting for another three decades.

Their resistance was ultimately defeated in 1793, when an alliance of Dutch mercenary troops and Ndyuka volunteers — bound by treaty obligation to support colonial forces against unrecognised Maroon groups — overcame them. This is one of the most complex chapters in Maroon history: the Ndyuka, having won their own freedom, were now legally required to help suppress the freedom struggle of others.

Following their defeat, the Aluku retreated across the Maroni River into what was then Dutch Guiana (later French Guiana). Over the following centuries their territory became anchored to the Lawa River, which forms the modern border between Suriname and French Guiana. When France established formal administrative control over French Guiana, the Aluku gradually came under French jurisdiction. Today the majority of Aluku hold French citizenship — making them the only Maroon group with EU citizenship.

This cross-border identity has created a distinctive dual existence. In French Guiana, the Aluku have experienced severe pressures from rapid development, gold mining by Brazilian garimpeiros, immigration from Haiti and Brazil, and French administrative policies that alternately ignored and disrupted their communities. In 2000, conflicts between Aluku gold miners and indigenous Wayana communities over mining operations came to a head. The economic and social situation in French Guiana contrasts sharply with Suriname but the threats to Aluku cultural survival are equally severe.

Boni, the great Aluku resistance leader, is commemorated across Suriname as a symbol of courage and defiance. The name Aluku itself means "those who were not afraid," and their 30-year war against overwhelming colonial force remains one of the most remarkable stories of Maroon resistance in the Americas.
