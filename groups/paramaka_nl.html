Paramaka

Also known as: Paamaka · Paramacca · Paramaccan

De Paramaka (Paamaka) zijn een Oostelijke Tak Marron-groep die het bekken van de Marowijne en de Lawa nabij de Surinaams-Frans-Guyana-grens bezetten. Ze spreken een variant van het Ndyuka en zijn historisch nauw verbonden geweest met de Ndyuka. Goudwinning is tegenwoordig de dominante economische activiteit in veel Paramaka-dorpen.

Geografisch Territorium

Primary territory — Marowijne & Lawa River basin, eastern Suriname

Kaart toont het Paramaka-territorium in het Marowijne-rivierbekken in oostelijk Suriname, grenzend aan Frans-Guyana. De Lawa (bovenste Marowijne) vormt een deel van de grens tussen Suriname en Frans-Guyana.

History

The Paramaka formed as a distinct Maroon community in the Marowijne River basin in the 18th century. They speak a variant of Ndyuka and have historically been closely associated with the Ndyuka people. During the first half of the 19th century, the Paramaka established formal relations with the Ndyuka and were in many respects treated as Ndyuka vassals, though they maintained their own identity, leadership structures, and territorial base.

Their territory lies along the Marowijne River, which forms the border between Suriname and French Guiana. This cross-border position has shaped Paramaka life in important ways — creating close ties with communities in French Guiana, and placing them in the middle of the complex politics surrounding movement across that border during both the gold rush era and the Interior War.

The Paramaka did not sign a formal peace treaty in the same manner as the Ndyuka, Saramaka, and Matawai. Their relationship with colonial and later national authorities was mediated largely through their association with the Ndyuka. This has created some ambiguity around their formal legal status and land rights, which, like all Maroon groups, remain unrecognised by the Surinamese state.

Today, many Paramaka men are involved in small-scale gold mining. The Minority Rights Group notes that Paramaka communities, alongside Aukan (Ndyuka) and Matawai communities, have become economically dependent on gold mining in some villages. Mercury contamination and associated health problems — malaria, sexually transmitted diseases, mercury poisoning — are documented concerns. A significant portion of the Paramaka population now lives in Paramaribo and in French Guiana.
