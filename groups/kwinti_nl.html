Kwinti

Also known as: Kwenti · Quinti

De Kwinti zijn de kleinste van de zes grote Marron-groepen, met een geschatte bevolking van circa 1.200 mensen. Ze zijn gevestigd langs de Coppename in centraal-west Suriname. Uniek onder de zes groepen hebben de Kwinti geen opperste granman — de enige Marron-groep zonder dit niveau van bestuur. Ze tekenden het laatste Marron-vredesverdrag met de Surinaamse autoriteiten in 1887, meer dan 125 jaar na het eerste.

History

The Kwinti are the smallest and least-documented of the six Maroon groups. They settled along the Coppename River in central-west Suriname — the most westerly of the six Maroon territories. Their relatively small population and geographic isolation have limited their visibility in historical records compared to the larger groups, yet their story is an integral part of the broader Maroon history of resistance and survival.

The Kwinti signed a formal peace treaty with Surinamese authorities in 1887 — the last of the six groups to do so, and more than 125 years after the first Ndyuka treaty. This long gap reflects both the Kwinti's geographic isolation in the western interior and the evolving relationship between the Surinamese colonial and national administration and its Maroon communities.

Uniquely among the six groups, the Kwinti have no paramount granman. Their governance structure relies instead on kapiteins governing individual communities, making their political organisation more decentralised than the other five groups. The reasons for this difference are not fully documented in the historical record, but it may reflect both the small size of the group and its geographic situation.

The Kwinti, like all Maroon groups, lost their treaty protections when Suriname abrogated the old peace treaties after independence in 1975. Their ancestral lands remain without formal legal recognition by the Surinamese state. The Interior War of 1986–1992, though centred in the east of the country, had ripple effects on Kwinti communities as the broader political crisis disrupted all interior Maroon life. Some Kwinti men are involved in small-scale gold mining; others have migrated to Paramaribo and the Netherlands.

Research on the Kwinti is relatively sparse. Academic anthropologists have focused primarily on the larger Ndyuka and Saramaka groups, and much of Kwinti oral history remains undocumented. This makes the Kwinti one of the most important communities for future research and cultural preservation efforts.
