Saramaka (Saamaka)

Also known as: Saamaka · Saramacca · Saramaccaner

The Saramaka are one of the two largest Maroon groups in Suriname, with a population roughly equal to the Ndyuka. Their traditional territory lies along the Upper Suriname River and its tributaries in south-central Suriname. In the 1960s, half their ancestral territory was flooded by the Brokopondo Reservoir — built to power an aluminium smelter — and thousands were forcibly relocated. In 2007 they won a landmark territorial rights ruling from the Inter-American Court of Human Rights.

History

The ancestors of the Saramaka escaped from Dutch plantations in the late 17th and early 18th centuries, coming from a variety of West and Central African peoples including the Akan, Bakongo, and Fon. They fled individually, in small groups, and sometimes in large collective rebellions into the dense rainforest, where for nearly a century they fought for their independence against Dutch colonial forces.

Their military effectiveness was such that 18th-century Dutch colonial maps showed defensive fortifications erected to protect coastal settlements from Saramaka raids. In 1762 — a full century before the general emancipation of slaves in Suriname — the Saramaka won their freedom through a peace treaty with the Dutch Crown, which acknowledged their territorial rights and trading privileges along the Upper Suriname River. The Saramaka preserve a very rich oral tradition about these formative years of resistance and escape.

In the 1960s, the construction of the Afobaka Dam to create the Brokopondo Reservoir — built to supply electricity to an aluminium smelter operated by Alcoa — flooded approximately half of traditional Saramaka territory and forced the relocation of thousands of people to government-built villages along the Lower Suriname River. The process was carried out with minimal consultation and wholly inadequate compensation. Today about one-third of all Saramaka live in French Guiana, most having migrated there since 1990 following the Interior War.

The 1986–1992 Interior War caused severe hardship. By mid-1989, approximately 3,000 Saramaka were living as temporary refugees in French Guiana. After the war, the national government largely neglected Saramaka communities while granting large timber and mining concessions to foreign multinationals — Chinese, Indonesian, Malaysian, and others — in traditional Saramaka territory, without consulting Saramaka authorities.

In 2000, the Association of Saramaka Authorities, representing 12 Saramaka clans and over 60 villages, petitioned the Inter-American Court of Human Rights, citing Chinese-owned lumber operations as a threat to their existence. The 2007 ruling determined that the Saramaka constitute a tribal people with rights over their traditionally occupied lands — a landmark decision for Maroon and Indigenous peoples worldwide. The Saramaka received compensation and control over a fund for their own development, but full implementation by the Surinamese government remains contested.
